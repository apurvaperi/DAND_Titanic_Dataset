Introduction¶

This project aims to perform a detailed data anlysis on the Titanic data set and draw conclusions on which passengers were more likely to survive the disaster and what factors contibuted to survival. The data has been obtained from https://www.kaggle.com/c/titanic

Here is a sample of the data, it is described in detail below.

Variable Definitions¶

survival : Survival --> 0 = No, 1 = Yes pclass : Ticket class --> 1 = 1st, 2 = 2nd, 3 = 3rd sex : Sex
Age : Age in years
sibsp : # of siblings / spouses aboard the Titanic
parch : # of parents / children aboard the Titanic
ticket : Ticket number
fare : Passenger fare
cabin : Cabin number
embarked : Port of Embarkation --> C = Cherbourg, Q = Queenstown, S = Southampton

Variable Notes¶

pclass: A proxy for socio-economic status (SES) 1st = Upper 2nd = Middle 3rd = Lower

age: Age is fractional if less than 1. If the age is estimated, is it in the form of xx.5

sibsp: The dataset defines family relations in this way... Sibling = brother, sister, stepbrother, stepsister Spouse = husband, wife (mistresses and fiancés were ignored)

parch: The dataset defines family relations in this way... Parent = mother, father Child = daughter, son, stepdaughter, stepson Some children travelled only with a nanny, therefore parch=0 for them.

Questions¶

Based on the above data, below are some questions I would like answered.

  1. What was the survival rate across the socio-economic classes?
  2. What was the survival rate across the sexes?
  3. Did port of embarkation have any effect on the survivability? (Were people from a certain port more suited to survive)
  4. Did children have a better chance at surviving than adults?
  5. How did age affect survivability in general?
  6. Did women from the upper class have a better chance of survival as compared to their lower class counterparts? How does this apply to the men on board?
  7. Were children without families (not just nannies) more likely to survive than those with families?

Here, survivability is the dependent variable and age, sex, socio-economic class, the presence of family are the independent variables for analysis.

# Function to Calculate the survival rate across all unique values for a given attribute. 
#This function can apply only to the Embarked, Sex and Pclass attributes
def survival_rate(attr):
    test = []
    value = pd.Series(titanic_data_raw[attr].unique())
    value = value[~value.isnull()]
    for val in value:
        total = float(len(titanic_data_raw[(titanic_data_raw[attr]== val)]))
        survived= float(len(titanic_data_raw[(titanic_data_raw[attr]== val) & (titanic_data_raw['Survived']==1)]))
        survival_rate = round(((survived/total)*100),2)
        test.append(survival_rate)
        print 'The survival rate for ' +repr(val)+ ' passengers was '  + repr(survival_rate) + ' %'
    return transpose(pd.DataFrame(test, index= value))

Conclusion¶

Based on my analysis so far, I can conclude the following:

  1. When looking at factors individually, women were more likely to survive with a survival rate of 74%, than men with a rate of only 18%. The same goes for passengers of higher socio-economic classes, with P1 passengers having a survival rate of 63 % and P3 having a survival rate of a mere 24%. While children might have had had a higher survival rate, beyond a point, age was not a big factor in determining survivability.
  2. The above effects are visible even when considering multiple independent factors. For instance, women from the 1st class had a much better chance of survival at 97% as compared to women from the 3rd class who had a survival rate of only 50%. Also, Passengers who embarked at Cherbourg had a significantly higher rate of survival at 51% as compared to those from the other ports because more than half of its passengers traveled 1st class.
  3. The influence of a family seems to have been consequential in the survival of children, with far more children with families surviving and those without families, dying. The same effect is not evident for adults with families, close to 70% of the adults without any family died. Further analysis highlights that this is because 95% of this group of adults were males with 62% of them traveling third class.

Assumptions and Limitations¶

  1. I have used an average imputation technique to fill in missing age data which can modify results especially my analysis of dependence of survivability on age. Ideal techniques would have been to extrapolate/ find a more accurate representation of the data.
  2. I have assumed passengers less than 18 years of age to be children. This is more of a modern day representation and may not accurately depict the age boundaries at the time of the Titanic.
  3. The dataset is only a sample and does not necessarily represent the entire population. This may have caused irregularities in my observations.
  4. There might even be other lurking variables like access/proximity to lifeboats, passenger location at the time of the accident and other such details which have not been taken into account in this analysis. This again may have caused skewed analysis or unaccountable behavior.
  5. The Titanic had crew members onboard as well and their chances of survival might have been different from that of the passengers. This data is not available in the dataset and limits the scope of the analysis in this project.
